Full Poem

If ever you should go by chance
To jungles in the East;
And if there should to you advance
A large and tawny beast,
If he roars at you as you’re dying,
You’ll know it is the Asian Lion…
Or if sometime when roaming round,
A noble wild beast greets you,
With black stripes on a yellow ground,
Just notice if he eats you.
This simple rule may help you learn
The Bengal Tiger to discern.
If strolling forth, a beast you view,
Whose hide with spots is peppered,
As soon as he has leapt on you,
You’ll know it is the Leopard.
’Twill do no good to roar with pain,
He’ll only lep and lep again.
If when you’re walking round your yard
You meet a creature there,
Who hugs you very, very hard,
Be sure it is a Bear.
If you have any doubts, I guess
He’ll give you just one more caress.
Though to distinguish beasts of prey
A novice might nonplus,
The Crocodile you always may
Tell from the Hyena thus:
Hyenas come with merry smiles;
But if they weep they’re Crocodiles.
The true Chameleon is small,
A lizard sort of thing;
He hasn’t any ears at all,
And not a single wing.
If there is nothing on the tree,
’Tis the chameleon you see.

Stanza-Wise Explanation

Stanza 1

If you go to the jungle and see a large, yellow-brown animal roaring loudly — and it scares you to death — that’s the Asian Lion.

Stanza 2

If you see an animal with black stripes on a yellow body that jumps and eats you — that's the Bengal Tiger.

Stanza 3

A spotted animal that leaps at you again and again and bites you is a Leopard.

Stanza 4

An animal that hugs you tightly and doesn't let go — it's a Bear showing affection (or attack).

Stanza 5

A Hyena smiles while the Crocodile cries. If it smiles, it’s a Hyena. If it cries, it’s a Crocodile.

Stanza 6

If you see nothing but feel something is there, it could be a Chameleon, which blends with its surroundings.

Hard Words & Meanings

Tawny: Yellowish-brown colour
Roars: Loud growl of lion
Discern: Identify or recognize
Peppered: Covered with
Lep: Old poetic form of "leap" (jump)
Caress: A loving or gentle touch
Prey: Animal that is hunted
Novice: A beginner
Nonplus: Confuse or puzzle
Chameleon: Lizard that changes colour

Poetic Devices

  • Alliteration: Repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words.
    Example: “he’ll only lep and lep again” (repetition of 'l')
  • Imagery: Creating mental pictures through words.
    Example: “A large and tawny beast”
  • Rhyme Scheme: Pattern of rhyming words (aabbcc in most stanzas)
    Example: “ground / round”, “you / too”
  • Personification: Giving human actions to animals.
    Example: “Hyenas come with merry smiles”
  • Humour: Funny or playful language.
    Example: “If he roars at you as you’re dying…”

Conclusion

This poem is a playful way to describe dangerous wild animals. The use of humour, rhyme, and vivid words makes it fun and easy for students to remember. It’s also a great example of how poetry can be both educational and entertaining.
