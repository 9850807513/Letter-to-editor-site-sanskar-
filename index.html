How to Tell Wild Animals

Seminar Presentation for Class 10

Introduction

"How to Tell Wild Animals" is a humorous poem written by Carolyn Wells. The poem tells us how to identify various wild animals in a funny and exaggerated way. The poet uses playful language and vivid imagery to describe animals like the Asian lion, Bengal tiger, leopard, and others.

Stanza-Wise Explanation

Stanza 1

The poet says if you go to the jungles of Asia and a large, tawny (yellowish-brown) beast roars loudly at you and you are dying because of fear, then you have met an Asian lion.

Stanza 2

If you are roaming in the forest and suddenly you see a wild animal with black stripes on a yellow body, and if it jumps on you and eats you, then it is the Bengal Tiger.

Stanza 3

If you spot a beast that is spotted and he pounces on you continuously, biting you hard, then it's a leopard. Crying will not help you because he will continue to bite.

Stanza 4

If you see a creature hugging you tightly and you feel like you are being squeezed to death, then it is probably a bear. The poet says you will easily know it is a bear by the way it hugs.

Stanza 5

The poet describes a crocodile as a creature that weeps while eating its prey. It is used humorously, as “crocodile tears” are fake tears.

Stanza 6

The chameleon is a small animal that changes its colour. The poet says if you see a tree with nothing on it, and yet you know there's something, then it's probably a chameleon blending in.

Hard Words and Their Meanings

Tawny: Yellowish-brown colour
Roars: Loud deep sound of a wild animal
Stripes: Long lines on animal body
Leopard: A wild cat with spots
Hugging: Holding tightly
Crocodile Tears: Fake or pretend tears
Chameleon: Lizard that changes its colour
Prey: Animal hunted by another

Poetic Devices

  • Alliteration: Repetition of consonant sounds at the beginning of words.
    Example: “Who hugs you very, very hard” (repetition of 'v')
  • Imagery: Use of vivid language to create mental pictures.
    Example: Description of the Bengal Tiger or Leopard
  • Rhyme Scheme: The pattern of rhyming words at the end of lines.
    Example: ababcdcd in most stanzas
  • Personification: Giving human traits to non-human things.
    Example: Crocodile shedding tears while eating
  • Humour: Funny and exaggerated situations
    Example: "If he roars at you and you are dying..."

Conclusion

"How to Tell Wild Animals" is a light-hearted and humorous poem that uses exaggeration, rhymes, and vivid imagery to describe wild animals in a fun way. It also helps students enjoy poetry while learning poetic devices and appreciating creative writing.
