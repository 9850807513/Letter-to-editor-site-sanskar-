How to Tell Wild Animals – Carolyn Wells

Stanza 1

If ever you should go by chance
To jungles in the east;
And if there should to you advance
A large and tawny beast,
If he roars at you as you’re dying,
You’ll know it is the Asian Lion…
Tawny: Yellowish-brown
Advance: Move forward
Roars: Loud deep sound made by lions
The poet says that if you go to a jungle in the East and see a large yellow-brown animal approaching and roaring loudly, you are meeting an Asian Lion.

Stanza 2

Or if some time when roaming round,
A noble wild beast greets you,
With black stripes on a yellow ground,
Just notice if he eats you.
This simple rule may help you learn
The Bengal Tiger to discern.
Roaming: Wandering
Noble: Majestic
Discern: Recognize
If you see a striped yellow animal and it eats you, it’s a Bengal Tiger. The poet humorously suggests you can only tell once it’s too late.

Stanza 3

If strolling forth, a beast you view,
Whose hide with spots is peppered,
As soon as he has leapt on you,
You’ll know it is the Leopard.
’Twill do no good to roar with pain,
He’ll only lep and lep again.
Peppered: Covered with
Strolling: Walking slowly
Lep: Leap (jump)
A spotted animal that jumps on you repeatedly is a Leopard. Screaming won't help, it will continue attacking.

Stanza 4

If when you’re walking round your yard
You meet a creature there,
Who hugs you very, very hard,
Be sure it is a Bear.
If you have any doubts, I guess
He’ll give you just one more caress.
Caress: Gentle touch
If a creature hugs you tightly, it might be a bear. If you're unsure, it may hug again — deadly but described humorously.

Stanza 5

Though to distinguish beasts of prey
A novice might nonplus,
The Crocodile you always may
Tell from the Hyena thus:
Hyenas come with merry smiles;
But if they weep they’re Crocodiles.
Nonplus: Confuse
Beasts of prey: Hunting animals
Novice: Beginner
Hyenas look like they’re laughing, crocodiles look like they’re crying. That’s the humorous way to tell them apart.

Stanza 6

The true Chameleon is small,
A lizard sort of thing;
He hasn’t any ears at all,
And not a single wing.
If there is nothing on the tree,
’Tis the chameleon you see.
Chameleon: A lizard that changes colour
'Tis: It is
Chameleons are hard to see because they blend with their surroundings. If you see nothing, it's probably a chameleon!

Poetic Devices (with definitions and examples)

Device Meaning Example from Poem
Alliteration Repetition of consonant sounds "he roars at you as you’re dying"
Imagery Words that create visual pictures "black stripes on a yellow ground"
Irony Opposite of expected meaning "Just notice if he eats you"
Personification Giving human traits to animals or things "He’ll give you one more caress"
Humour Use of funny or playful language Used throughout the poem
